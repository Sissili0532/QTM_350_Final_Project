Title: Impact of Education and GDP on Adolescent Fertility¶

Authors: Anna Huang, Rola Ke, Sissi Li, Vicki Lin, Chrissy Wang¶

Research Question: Do higher education levels and GDP per capita contribute to lower adolescent fertility rates across countries?¶

INTRODUCTION :¶

The relationship between economic prosperity and demographic outcomes has long been a focal point of global development studies. Adolescent fertility is a critical indicator of both gender equality and social mobility, often reflecting the accessibility of human capital resources like education. This project investigates how national wealth (GDP per capita) and educational attainment influence the reproductive health landscapes of six diverse nations: Brazil, Switzerland, Italy, Malaysia, Niger, and India.

These countries were selected to represent a broad spectrum of the global development hierarchy. Switzerland and Italy represent high-income, established economies with robust social infrastructures and high educational enrollment. Brazil and Malaysia serve as middle-income emerging powers navigating significant socio-economic transitions. In contrast, India and Niger represent developing contexts; India is a rapidly expanding economy with heterogeneous development outcomes, while Niger faces unique demographic challenges, consistently recording some of the highest fertility rates globally. By comparing these varying structures, we aim to understand the extent to which wealth and schooling act as buffers against early childbearing.

DATA DESCRIPTION: ¶

We utilize data from the World Bank’s World Development Indicators (WDI) database to conduct this analysis. We specifically focus on three primary indicators to serve as proxies for the economic and social health of the nations:

  • GDP per capita (current US$): This metric provides a view of individual economic wellbeing and the total economic resources available per person. It allows us to compare the level of national wealth and its potential impact on public health and social services.

  • Secondary Education Enrollment (gross %): This indicator measures the level of human capital development. It offers insight into the accessibility of
    formal schooling for adolescents, which is a critical factor in delaying childbearing and increasing economic opportunity for young women.

  • Adolescent Fertility Rate (births per 1,000 women ages 15-19): This variable measures the frequency of childbearing among the youth population. It serves as a primary proxy for reproductive health outcomes and the effectiveness of social support systems.

In addition to these, we include Primary and Education Enrollment as a supplemented indicator to provide a deeper dimension into the foundational educational stability of these nations. The dataset covers the period from 2020 to 2024 (5-year-period), allowing us to observe these trends in the current post-pandemic global economy.

RESEARCH OBJECTIVE:¶

The primary objective of this analysis is to quantify the relationship between socio-economic development and reproductive health outcomes across diverse national contexts.

Specifically, this project addresses the following research questions: How do GDP per capita and secondary education enrollment rates influence adolescent fertility trends in nations with varying economic structures?

Furthermore, which of these indicators—wealth or education—shows a stronger correlation with reduced fertility rates? Through comparative descriptive statistics and correlation analysis, this report seeks to identify the key drivers that contribute to lower adolescent fertility and to map the developmental trajectories of the six selected countries.

Interpretation:¶

  1. Primary Education: The Foundation (High Saturation)

The first pane shows that nearly all countries—except Niger—have achieved high primary enrollment (above 90%).

The Insight: Because Brazil, India, Malaysia, Italy, and Switzerland all cluster at the high end of primary school enrollment, this variable alone doesn't explain why their fertility rates differ so much. Primary education is a "baseline" requirement, but it isn't enough to drive adolescent fertility toward zero.

  1. Secondary Education: The Critical Pivot (Strongest Correlation)

This is your most "telling" graph. You can see the countries begin to spread out significantly across the X-axis.

The Insight: There is a clear, steep diagonal relationship here. As enrollment moves from 20% (Niger) to 80% (India/Malaysia) to 100%+ (Italy/Switzerland), the fertility rate drops predictably. This suggests that keeping girls in school through their teenage years is the most effective deterrent to early childbirth among these variables.

  1. Tertiary Education: The Developed World Luxury

In the final pane, we see a massive "data gap."

The Missing Link: Niger is absent here because tertiary data for the 2020–2024 period is often unavailable for ultra-low-income nations.

The Insight: For the countries that do have data, the trend line flattens significantly. This indicates that by the time a population reaches tertiary (college) level, adolescent fertility is already extremely low. Tertiary education is more of a characteristic of "High GDP" nations rather than a direct driver for reducing adolescent births (since most tertiary students are already past the age of 19).

Therefore, a comparative analysis of educational tiers suggests that Secondary Education Enrollment provides the most significant explanatory power for variations in adolescent fertility. While primary education has reached near-saturation in most studied nations, the divergence in secondary enrollment highlights a critical 'protection' window. The transition from primary to secondary school marks the point where economic opportunity and reproductive health outcomes most clearly align.

This suggests that educational policy may be a more direct and effective lever for social change than general economic growth alone.

The regression line is nearly flat, with most countries clustered between 90% and 110% enrollment (the >100% values usually represent over-age students or repeaters).

Primary education is no longer a "luxury" tied to high GDP. Almost every nation in your study, from India to Switzerland, has hit near-saturation. Niger is the only clear outlier here, suggesting that at the lowest levels of wealth, even basic education access remains a struggle.

  1. GDP vs. Secondary Education: The Linear Engine

This pane shows the most consistent upward trend across the middle-income band.

This is the most "equitable" relationship. As nations move from low to middle income (moving from India/Niger toward Brazil/Malaysia/Italy), they see the most direct return on their wealth in terms of secondary school seats. This confirms that secondary education is the "bridge" that wealth builds to improve social outcomes.

  1. GDP vs. Tertiary Education: The High-Income Differentiator

Unlike the other two plots, the tertiary graph shows significant "vertical" spread even among countries with lower GDPs.

Tertiary enrollment remains a marker of high-income stability. While Italy and Switzerland are far to the right, you can see that nations like India and Malaysia have very different tertiary outcomes despite being relatively close in GDP per capita. This suggests that tertiary education is highly dependent on specific national policy and infrastructure, not just raw economic output.

Therefore, the Primary Education trajectory serves as a nearly universal prerequisite, acting as the "floor" of development; the flat slope indicates that once a nation moves past extreme poverty, basic enrollment becomes a non-negotiable standard that is relatively independent of further GDP increases. In contrast, Secondary Education acts as the study's "economic engine," displaying the most sensitive and consistent slope where incremental gains in GDP per capita translate directly into higher enrollment rates. Finally, Tertiary Education functions as a development "ceiling" or luxury, where the wide vertical dispersion across the graph suggests that advanced educational attainment is the result of long-term accumulated wealth and specific policy choices rather than immediate economic output.

Findings from the analysis:¶

  1. The Wealth-Education Pipeline

The analysis confirms that economic prosperity is a primary driver of educational infrastructure, but its impact varies by level. While Primary Education has reached a saturation point across nearly all income levels, Secondary Education shows the most direct linear growth in response to GDP gains. This suggests that as nations like India and Brazil transition into higher income brackets, their most significant "human capital" shift occurs at the secondary level.

  1. Education as the Primary Social Lever

While GDP and fertility are negatively correlated, the relationship between Secondary Education and Adolescent Fertility is statistically more profound (r=−0.86). The data indicates a "protection effect": nations that maintain high secondary enrollment rates consistently report the lowest adolescent birth rates, regardless of their absolute wealth. This identifies education as a more direct determinant of reproductive health outcomes than raw economic output.

  1. Developmental Divergence

The "Log-Scale" and "Educational Threshold" plots highlight a significant gap between the High-Income Stability of Switzerland and Italy and the Structural Stagnation of Niger. The findings suggest that there is a critical "transition zone" (exemplified by Malaysia and India) where targeted investments in secondary schooling can lead to a disproportionate drop in adolescent fertility, potentially breaking the cycle of poverty.

Conclusion¶

Based on the descriptive evidence, Secondary Education Enrollment is the most influential factor in reducing adolescent fertility rates. While GDP per capita provides the necessary financial foundation to build and sustain school systems, it is the actual attainment of secondary education that acts as the functional mechanism for lowering fertility. Therefore, economic growth is most effective at improving social outcomes when it is specifically channeled into teenage educational retention.
